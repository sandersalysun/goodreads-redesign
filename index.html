goodreads app redesign

How might we improve the experience of students using the Goodreads app on campus?

why goodreads?


As both a Goodreads user and a UX designer, I’ve experienced my fair share of frustrations with the current app—its interface feels outdated, uninviting, and incredibly slow. Why is this the go-to app for book lovers to share their recent reads? Can’t we do better? So, when I was asked to redesign an app interface for my Interactive Design class, I knew Goodreads was overdue for an upgrade.

I set out to create a new and improved Goodreads. To do this, I needed to gather research to make sure I appealed to the needs of the user.



Current Goodreads Interface




user research


In the research stage, the goal is to empathize with users by fully understanding their needs, behaviors, and pain points through direct engagement. Here’s how I approached this:

I began my user research by generating 8 open-ended research questions using AI.


Here is how I prompted my chatbot to formulate my interview questions:



research questions


1) How do you feel about the overall look and feel of the Goodreads app? What, if anything, would you change about the interface?

2) How would you improve the process of discovering new books or managing your reading lists on Goodreads?

3) Can you walk me through how you typically use Goodreads? Are there any features you avoid because they are difficult to use?

4) Can you describe a recent experience where the design of the app made it harder or easier for you to navigate?


5) Are there any features you wish Goodreads had that would make it more useful for your studies or campus life?

6) How intuitive do you find the navigation in the Goodreads app? Are there any areas where you get stuck or confused?

7) What would an ideal version of GoodReads look like to you? What changes would make the app more user-friendly and easier to navigate?

8) How do you feel about Goodreads as a platform for connecting with other readers? What would make those connections stronger or more engaging for you?



For survey responses, I found which of my peers were readers and whether they used or once used the Goodreads app. I limited my search to college students. For majority of peers, I sent the research questions via Google Forms. For the the peers that were most familiar with the Goodreads interface, I conducted an in-person interview. I also conducted an interview with AI. I distinguished the human feedback from the AI feedback in my interview findings.

interview findings


Navigation Difficulties:

Many users, both in surveys and interviews, find Goodreads confusing to navigate. They report frequent misclicks, unclear pathways, and the need to click through many menus to get to desired features.

Users noted how the app feels glitchy, leading to frustration. Megan, Sam, and Summer expressed confusion over the cluttered homepage, often showing them irrelevant information (like trending books).

It emphasized confusion in managing bookshelves and a lack of intuitive navigation, though it acknowledged the main features are clear.

Outdated Interface:

The app’s look is consistently criticized for being unattractive, outdated, and bland.

Megan and Sam described the interface as “ugly” and “beige,” while Nathan commented that the app feels stuck in the past, “like it hasn’t been updated since 2015.”

The AI also highlighted the outdated design and suggested using more whitespace and modern typography.

Glitchy Experience:

Almost all users found the app glitchy and slow to load, which made the user experience frustrating.

Megan and Summer frequently gave up when the app glitched. Sam also mentioned how the slowness made the app less enjoyable to use.

The AI mirrored these concerns, describing the interface as "clunky," impacting task completion speed.

Challenges in Connecting with Friends:

Most users mentioned issues with the social features, particularly connecting with friends.

Nathan and Sam were both frustrated with the difficulty in finding and interacting with friends. Megan and Summer found the process cumbersome, with Summer unable to even find friends using their exact username.

ChatGPT echoed this sentiment, calling for stronger social features like chat or discussion rooms to foster better connections.

Improvement Suggestions:

A common suggestion was to add more customization to profiles, possibly including themes or a dedicated landing page (Megan, Summer).


Nathan and Sam would like to see Goodreads incorporate more interactive features, like communication tools (e.g., chat) or book clubs, similar to other apps like Letterboxd.
Many noted the need for a better recommendation algorithm that could reflect their true reading preferences rather than focusing solely on trending or popular books.

The AI also called for improved recommendations, more intuitive filters, and better social interaction features like book clubs and chat.



survey vs. interview findings


Surveys:

More respondents (7/12) found the app’s cluttered homepage an issue and preferred the ability to customize recommendations. Others called for ways to group reading lists or better manage bookshelves.

Interviews:

The live interviews brought up some unique pain points:

  • Megan suggested logging individual chapters of textbooks for academic purposes, while Summer wanted to link Goodreads with her school email.
  • Sam and Nathan expressed dissatisfaction with the app’s algorithm, noting its tendency to recommend only the most popular books, which sometimes clashed with their personal preferences.

From these survey results, I needed to identify the most common pain points communicated by users. I used a chatbot to help identify the five most communicated points of pain from users. Using AI in this process helped me eliminate any bias I may have held about the app. After identifying these pain points, I needed to make sure I addressed these issues in my design requirements.


five pain points


Here is how I prompted my chatbot to help me identify five pain points based on the needs communicated by users:




1. Difficult Navigation:

Users consistently reported difficulty navigating the app. Many had to click through multiple menus to reach desired features, and they found that certain sections (like profile pages or reading lists) were buried deep in the interface.

2. Outdated Interface:

Users frequently mention the outdated design, with a bland and cluttered appearance that lacks modern UI features like dark mode. Both surveys and interviews indicated that the app looks "dated" and "ugly," with a beige color palette and rigid, sharp squares contributing to an unpleasant visual experience.

3. Poor Social Features:

A recurring theme was dissatisfaction with the social features, specifically how difficult it is to connect with friends. Users also felt that once they connected, there was little they could do with those connections, making the social experience passive and unengaging.

4. Overwhelming and Poor Discoverability:

The app overwhelms users with features, and finding new books is difficult due to impersonal and inaccurate recommendations that are not tailored to users reading preferences.

5. Slowness and Glitches:

Many users, particularly in the interviews, complained about frequent glitches and long load times, which sometimes caused them to give up on the app entirely.

ux prototyping

Before designing my prototypes, I wanted to challenge my previous assumptions I made about the Goodreads app based on what the results presented in the data.

challenge assumptions + ideate


I assumed the app’s design, while somewhat outdated, wouldn’t be a major pain point for users. I thought minor aesthetic updates like color tweaks or small UI changes would suffice in making the app more appealing.

The research revealed that the design was a significant issue for most users. They found the interface not just dated but unpleasant to use, describing it as "ugly" and "beige." Many users explicitly mentioned the lack of modern UI features like dark mode, clearer visuals, and better use of whitespace. The design seemed to have a bigger impact on usability and user satisfaction than expected.

I expanded my ideation to include a complete design overhaul rather than small updates. Ideas like incorporating more whitespace, modern typography, and offering customizable dark mode were central to improving the overall aesthetic and user engagement. I also considered a more fluid, rounded design to replace the rigid and boxy UI that users disliked.

I thought that Goodreads' recommendation system was at least satisfactory for most users, as it uses data-driven algorithms based on books added to shelves, ratings, and reviews. I expected that most complaints would center around other app features.

The research contradicted this assumption, as users found the recommendation system impersonal, mostly offering trending books or popular reads rather than truly reflecting their unique reading preferences. This resulted in frustration when trying to discover new books.

Ideation focused on improving the recommendation algorithm to better align with individual user preferences, taking into account reading history, genre preferences, and possibly incorporating friend recommendations to add a social element to book discovery. Rather than recommending popular titles, the system would become more personalized and refined.

Since the primary function of Goodreads is to track reading progress, I assumed that users would find tasks like adding books or managing their reading lists straightforward and intuitive.

Users actually struggled with managing their bookshelves and adding books. They expressed frustration over the process being unintuitive, with tasks like adding books, editing activity, and organizing reading lists being more cumbersome than expected. They wanted more control over categorizing books and managing private vs. public shelves.

Based on this, I ideated around batch management of bookshelves and more intuitive controls. For instance, users could easily create and customize multiple reading categories (school vs. leisure books) with private and public settings, reducing the clutter and improving book organization.

chatbot design


Willy the Bookworm, my AI chatbot for the Goodreads app, encourages social engagement by suggesting and helping form book clubs, proactively suggesting interactions, surfacing relevant content, and streamlining social features like commenting, group invitations, and private messaging.


willy's capabilities


1) commenting assistance

  • Suggests quick comments when users view friends' activity (e.g., “I loved this book too!” or “What did you think of the ending?”)
  • Provides a shortcut for users to leave comments directly within notifications about friends’ activities (status updates, reviews, book completions).
  • Tracks user preferences for commenting and offers personalized recommendations (e.g., showing most active friends first).

2) activity feed integration

  • Notifies users when friends update their reading statuses, review books, or join new groups, with options to quickly react or comment.
  • Displays group activity, highlighting interesting discussions happening in book clubs that match the user’s interests.
  • Allows users to easily join group discussions or start new ones based on trending topics in their circles.

3) social suggestion

  • Recommends friends based on shared reading preferences or recent interactions (e.g., if two users are reading the same book).
  • Suggests book clubs or group activities aligned with the user’s recent book history or genre preferences.
  • Suggests friends or users to follow based on similar activity patterns (e.g., mutual reviews, joining the same group).

4) bookclub engagement

  • Sends reminders about upcoming book club meetings or discussions.
  • Offers summaries of book club conversations and suggests where to chime in.
  • Enables users to start book discussions from a chat with their friends, turning private conversations into public group topics.

5) personalized discussion prompts

  • Uses AI to generate meaningful discussion prompts based on users' reading habits or recent book reviews (e.g., “Want to ask your friends what they thought about this twist?”).
  • Proposes discussion topics or polls within group chats to foster engagement and participation.

6) friend's activity summary

  • Provides daily or weekly summaries of friends' activities and suggests ways to interact (e.g., "Audrey finished reading 1984, do you want to ask her how she liked it?”).
  • Compiles friends’ reading progress and reviews for quick browsing.

7) direct messaging enhancement

  • Allows users to message friends directly from book pages or activity notifications.
  • Integrates with other messaging services for quick sharing (e.g., sharing book quotes or reviews).

a/b testing

Before finalizing my app design, I wanted to do an A/B test to make sure my flows were as user-friendly as possible and did not cause any extra complications. I decided to test my second user flow: discovering and adding a book to shelf.

I used Chat GPT to help me brainstorm some alternative user flows for this task. Here is how I prompted my chatbot.



why this flow was chosen for testing


This user flow -discovering a new book and adding it to a shelf— is critical for user engagement on a platform like Goodreads. Many users rely on Goodreads to track their reading and organize books, so ensuring this process is as smooth and intuitive as possible can significantly impact user satisfaction.

Key reasons for choosing this flow for testing:

  • Frequency of Use: Adding books to shelves is a common action that users perform frequently. Any improvement here could have a big impact on the app's usability and the user’s overall experience.
  • Reducing Friction: Interviews and surveys showed that users felt overwhelmed by too many options or steps, leading to friction in the book-adding process. Testing a streamlined option (Version B) can reveal if a quicker, more focused flow leads to greater satisfaction.
  • Balancing Simplicity and Functionality: Version A offers more options upfront, but it may clutter the interface and slow down the process. Version B reduces visual clutter and simplifies the action, which may be more efficient but needs to be tested to ensure it doesn’t sacrifice functionality.


differences between version (a) and version (b)


Book Details Page Layout

Multiple buttons are displayed on the Book Details page, allowing users to directly choose specific actions like “Currently Reading” or “Bookclub Read.” This version gives users more options upfront but can look cluttered and may require more taps.

Only one “Quick Add” button is present, streamlining the interface and reducing visual clutter. Users can access other actions, but these are hidden behind a modal for a cleaner look.

Adding to Shelves Interaction

Tapping “Add to Shelves” leads the user to a list of all their shelves. They can scroll through all shelves and check one or more to add the book. This method provides full control but requires more steps.

Tapping “Quick Add” brings up a modal with personalized shelf options based on the user’s habits (e.g., “Want to Read,” “Favorites”). Users can quickly tap one of these options without having to scroll through a long list. Additional shelves are accessible but hidden behind a scroll or “see more” option, offering more simplicity for common actions.

Confirmation and Feedback

After selecting shelves, the user might receive a confirmation message or visual feedback to confirm the book was added.

After a single tap on a shelf in the modal, a brief confirmation (like a checkmark animation or toast message) appears to indicate that the book was successfully added, then the modal closes automatically.



testing hypothesis


The A/B test will examine whether simplifying the process with Version B (single “Quick Add” button and a modal with pre-selected shelves) improves user experience by making the flow faster and more intuitive, without sacrificing user satisfaction. To conduct the test, I had 5 users complete user flow design (a) and 5 users complete user flow design (b). Immediately after completing the task, the users answered 5 follow up questions depending on which version they were given.


Hypothesis: Users will prefer Version B because it reduces the number of steps, presents a cleaner interface, and aligns with the need for a quick and convenient way to organize books.

Metrics: User satisfaction will be measured using a post-interaction feedback survey on ease of use and enjoyment.

By comparing these metrics, you can assess which version better supports users' needs and offers a smoother experience for organizing books on Goodreads.

I used Chat GPT to help me analyze the data I received from users and summarize my results to help me better identify patters and an overall unbiased consensus from the data. Here is how I prompted my chatbot:




results from user flow (a)


Ease of Use: Most users found the interface relatively intuitive, but many noted that they had to pause to ensure they selected the correct button. There was a common sentiment that the screen felt cluttered and that the multiple options could be overwhelming.

Shelf Selection Process: Users generally found it easy to select shelves but felt that the multiple steps involved added complexity. A few users mentioned that the extra options (like adding to multiple shelves) were beneficial, although some thought they weren’t necessary for their typical use.

Control and Flexibility: The option to add to multiple shelves was appreciated by some, but several users felt that it wasn’t necessary for every use case. The sentiment was split—some users valued the control, while others found it excessive.

Suggestions for Improvement: Users suggested reducing steps, improving the visual hierarchy of buttons, and making the "Add to Shelves" button more prominent.


results from user flow (b)


Ease of Use: All users found the Quick Add button easy to understand and appreciate the simplicity. Many described the process as “streamlined” and “clear,” with fewer opportunities for confusion compared to Version A.

Shelf Relevance: Most users were happy with the suggested shelves in the modal. However, some noted that it would be beneficial if they could customize which shelves appear in the Quick Add options based on their needs.

Flexibility: Users generally did not find the limited number of shelves restrictive. The "See More" button was reassuring for those who wanted to access less-used shelves, indicating that additional options were available if needed.

Preference for Streamlined Interface: Users overwhelmingly preferred the Quick Add approach, mentioning that it minimized decision fatigue and provided a more convenient experience.



did my hypothesis test true?


Yes,the hypothesis that users would prefer Version B due to its simplicity and convenience proved to be accurate. The majority of users expressed a preference for the streamlined Quick Add functionality in Version B, as it reduced the number of steps and presented a cleaner interface.


User satisfaction was higher for Version B, with users describing it as "easy," "streamlined," and "clear." Version A had mixed reviews; while some users valued the control it provided, others found it overwhelming or felt that certain options weren’t necessary for their use case. Overall, users seemed more content with the convenience and efficiency of Version B.



did you gain any unexpected insights?


Some users suggested that being able to customize which shelves appeared in the Quick Add modal would further enhance usability. This was an interesting suggestion that wasn’t anticipated and could potentially make the flow even more personalized.


Another insight was that a few users in Version A liked having the option to add to multiple shelves, even if they didn’t use it often. This implies that a balance of simplicity with flexible options could be beneficial in future designs.


One user noted that it would be helpful to see which friends have read the book, hinting at a potential social integration element that could add value to the experience.



takeaways using AI


This was my first time using AI so extensively for a UX project and I was very impressed by its ability to streamline the research process, especially when it came to finding patterns in data. As designers, we can be biased, and using AI helped me see past my preferences and understand what the users were telling me based on their responses. In the future, I plan to continue using AI for data driven projects such as this one and would encourage my future employers to implement it into their own UX/UI design processes as well.
